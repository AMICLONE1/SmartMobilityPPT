PARAMETERS

Key Parameter: Capacity (Q)

The total charge a cell can deliver from full to empty, measured in Ampere-hours (Ah).

Q = ∫0t I(t) dt     [Ah]

A cell rated 3 Ah can deliver 3A for 1 hour, or 1.5A for 2 hours, or 6A for 30 minutes (approximately — real capacity depends on C-rate and temperature).

Why This Matters The shape of this curve determines how well the OCV method works. NMC has a nice slope throughout. LFP is nearly flat from 20–80% — making OCV almost useless in that range.

PARAMETERS

Key Parameter: Internal Resistance (R₀)

Caused by electrolyte ionic resistance, contact resistance, and current collectors. Creates an instantaneous voltage drop when current flows.

Vdrop = I × R₀

For a cell with R₀ = 20 mΩ at 10A discharge:

Vdrop = 10 × 0.02 = 0.2V

This 200mV drop means the terminal voltage is lower than OCV during discharge, and higher than OCV during charge. The BMS must account for this.

Chemistry Shapes

  • NMC: Nice S-curve, good slope everywhere → OCV works well
  • LFP: Very flat 20–80% → OCV nearly useless in mid-range
  • NCA: Steep curve → highly sensitive to voltage noise
  • LTO: Low voltage (2.3V), moderate slope

OCV METHOD

Experimental Method 1: Low-Rate Discharge

Discharge at very low C-rate (C/20 or C/25) to minimize polarization effects.

1Fully charge the cell to 100% SOC using CC-CV protocol to the manufacturer's upper cutoff voltage.
2Discharge at C/25 rate (e.g., 0.12A for a 3Ah cell). This very slow discharge minimizes internal resistance effects.
3Record voltage vs. accumulated Ah at regular intervals (every 1% SOC or every minute).
4Repeat for charging direction. Average the discharge and charge curves to get the equilibrium OCV.
Why C/25? At C/25, the voltage drop I×R₀ = 0.12A × 0.02Ω = 2.4mV, which is negligible compared to OCV changes (~1.2V total range). So terminal voltage ≈ OCV.
OCV METHOD

Experimental Method 2: GITT (Pulse Discharge)

Galvanostatic Intermittent Titration Technique — pulse then rest, pulse then rest.

1Fully charge. Apply 1C current pulse for 6 minutes (= 10% SOC step).
2Rest for 1–2 hours until voltage stabilizes. Record this stable voltage as OCV at that SOC.
3Repeat for each 10% step from 100% to 0%. Total: ~20+ hours of testing.
Bonus The GITT data also lets you extract R₀, R₁, C₁ parameters for the equivalent circuit model — needed for the Kalman filter later!

OCV METHOD

Mathematical Representation: Polynomial Fit

The OCV-SOC relationship is typically fit with a 6th–8th order polynomial:

OCV(SOC) = a₀ + a₁·SOC + a₂·SOC² + a₃·SOC³ + ... + aₙ·SOCⁿ

Where SOC is normalized (0 to 1), and coefficients a₀ through aₙ are determined by least-squares fitting to experimental data.

MATLAB Command Use p = polyfit(SOC_data, OCV_data, 7) to get a 7th-order polynomial fit. Then OCV_fit = polyval(p, SOC_vector) to evaluate.
OCV METHOD

Mathematical Representation: Nernst-Based Model

A physically motivated model combining logarithmic terms (from the Nernst equation) with polynomial correction:

OCV(SOC) = K₀ + K₁/SOC + K₂·SOC + K₃·ln(SOC) + K₄·ln(1−SOC)

Why Logarithmic Terms?

The Nernst equation from electrochemistry predicts logarithmic voltage behavior at extreme SOC values. The ln(SOC) term captures the steep drop near 0%, and ln(1−SOC) captures the steep rise near 100%.

Advantage Over Polynomial

Polynomials struggle with steep endpoints (Runge's phenomenon). The Nernst-based model naturally handles this with fewer parameters and better extrapolation behavior.

OCV METHOD

Temperature Dependence of OCV

OCV shifts with temperature. For NMC: +0.1 to +0.5 mV/°C. This can cause 2–5% SOC error if ignored.

OCV(SOC, T) = OCVref(SOC) + α · (T − Tref)

Option A: Multiple Curves

Store separate OCV-SOC tables at -10°C, 0°C, 25°C, 45°C. Interpolate between them based on measured temperature.

Option B: Temperature Offset

Store one reference curve (at 25°C) and apply a linear temperature correction factor α.

Typical α For NMC: α ≈ 0.3 mV/°C. For LFP: α ≈ 0.1 mV/°C (smaller because LFP voltage is inherently more stable).
OCV METHOD

Hysteresis Effect (Especially LFP)

For some chemistries, OCV during charging is slightly higher than during discharging at the same SOC.

OCVactual(SOC) = OCVavg(SOC) ± h(SOC)/2

KALMAN FILTER

ECM Voltage Equation

Vterminal = OCV(SOC) − I × R₀ − VRC

Terminal voltage equals OCV minus the two voltage drops: instantaneous (R₀) and dynamic (V_RC from the RC pair).

KALMAN FILTER

ECM: V_RC Dynamics (RC Pair)

dVRC/dt = −VRC / (R₁×C₁) + I / C₁

This is a first-order ODE. V_RC charges up when current flows and decays with time constant τ₁ = R₁×C₁ when current stops. This models the slow voltage relaxation you see after removing load.

KALMAN FILTER

State Variables Definition

The EKF needs a state vector — the quantities we're estimating:

x = [SOC, VRC]T    (2×1 state vector)

SOC is what we want. V_RC is a "hidden" state — we can't measure it directly, but we need it to predict terminal voltage accurately.

KALMAN FILTER

State Equation: SOC Update

Discretize the SOC equation with sample period Δt:

SOC[k+1] = SOC[k] − (Δt / (Q × 3600)) × I[k]

This is just Coulomb counting! The prediction step IS Coulomb counting. The magic of EKF is what comes after — the correction step.

KALMAN FILTER

State Equation: V_RC Update

Discretize the V_RC ODE using zero-order hold:

VRC[k+1] = e−Δt/τ₁ × VRC[k] + R₁ × (1 − e−Δt/τ₁) × I[k]

The exponential term e−Δt/τ₁ is the decay factor. With Δt=1s and τ₁=20s: e−1/20 = 0.951.

KALMAN FILTER

Compact Matrix Form

x[k+1] = A × x[k] + B × I[k] + w[k]
A = [1, 0;  0, e−Δt/τ₁]
B = [−Δt/(Q×3600);  R₁×(1−e−Δt/τ₁)]

w[k] = process noise with covariance matrix Qnoise.

KALMAN FILTER

Output (Measurement) Equation

Vterminal[k] = OCV(SOC[k]) − R₀ × I[k] − VRC[k] + v[k]

This predicts what the voltage should be based on our state estimate. v[k] = measurement noise with variance Rnoise.

Nonlinearity OCV(SOC) is nonlinear. This is why we need the Extended Kalman Filter — it linearizes around the current estimate.

KALMAN FILTER

Why "Extended" Kalman Filter?

The standard KF assumes linear models. Our output equation has OCV(SOC) which is nonlinear. The EKF handles this by computing a Jacobian (local linearization) at each step.

C[k] = [∂OCV/∂SOC |SOC=predicted,  −1]

The term ∂OCV/∂SOC is the slope of the OCV-SOC curve at the current predicted SOC.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 1: PREDICT — State

Phase 1a: Predict State

x̂⁻[k] = A × x̂[k−1] + B × I[k−1]

Propagate the state forward using the battery model. This is the "Coulomb counting + V_RC prediction" step.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 1: PREDICT — Covariance

Phase 1b: Predict Covariance

P⁻[k] = A × P[k−1] × AT + Qnoise

Propagate the uncertainty forward. P grows because the model is imperfect.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 2: UPDATE — Jacobian

Phase 2a: Compute Jacobian

C[k] = [dOCV/dSOC |SOC=SOĈ⁻,  −1]

Linearize the output equation around the current predicted SOC. Use the dOCV_dSOC lookup table.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 2: UPDATE — Kalman Gain

Phase 2b: Kalman Gain

K[k] = P⁻[k] × CT × (C × P⁻ × CT + Rnoise)−1

K determines how much to trust the measurement. Large K = trust measurement. Small K = trust model.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 2: UPDATE — Innovation

Phase 2c: Innovation (Measurement Residual)

ỹ[k] = Vmeasured − (OCV(SOĈ⁻) − R₀ × I − VRĈ⁻)

The difference between what we measured and what the model predicted. If ỹ is large, our estimate is off.

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 2: UPDATE — State Correction

Phase 2d: State Update

x̂[k] = x̂⁻[k] + K[k] × ỹ[k]

Correct the predicted state using the Kalman gain × innovation. This is where the self-correction happens!

KALMAN FILTER

EKF Phase 2: UPDATE — Covariance Update

Phase 2e: Covariance Update

P[k] = (I − K[k] × C[k]) × P⁻[k]

After incorporating the measurement, our uncertainty decreases. P shrinks, reflecting increased confidence in the estimate.

KALMAN FILTER

The Kalman Gain Intuition

The Kalman gain K automatically balances trust:

📈

Model Uncertain (large P)

K is large → correction is large → "trust the measurement more"

📉

Measurement Noisy (large R)

K is small → correction is small → "trust the model more"

Analogy Like a GPS that combines inertial sensors (model=Coulomb counting) with satellite fixes (measurement=voltage). In a tunnel (no GPS), rely on inertial. In open sky, GPS corrects drift.
KALMAN FILTER

Tuning: Process Noise Q

Qnoise = diag(qSOC, qVRC). Represents model imperfection.

q value Effect
Large (1e-3) Filter adapts fast, but noisy estimate
Small (1e-6) Smooth estimate, but slow to correct errors
Typical start: 1e-5 Good balance for most applications

KALMAN FILTER

Tuning: Measurement Noise R

Rnoise = voltage measurement variance.

Rnoise ≈ (σvoltage)² ≈ (0.01V)² = 10−4 V²

For a 12-bit ADC measuring 0–5V: resolution = 5/4096 ≈ 1.2mV. Noise σ ≈ 5–10mV is typical.

KALMAN FILTER

Tuning: Initial Covariance P₀

P₀ represents initial uncertainty.

Scenario P₀(SOC) P₀(V_RC)
SOC from OCV (confident) 0.01 0.01
SOC guessed 0.25 0.01

REGEN BRAKING

Fundamental Principle

In conventional braking, kinetic energy → heat (wasted). In an EV, the motor reverses: kinetic energy → electrical energy → battery.

PowerNetPro Pvt. Ltd.
59
Omkar · EV Centre of Excellence, MIT-WPU | PowerNetPro Pvt.Ltd.
REGEN BRAKING

Normal Force Equations

Nfront = m·g·(Lr/L) + m·|a|·(hcg/L)
Nrear = m·g·(Lf/L) − m·|a|·(hcg/L)

L = wheelbase, L_f/L_r = CG distances, h_cg = CG height. Front load increases, rear load decreases during braking.

REGEN BRAKING

Maximum Braking Force (Tire Limit)

Fbrake_max_front = μ × Nfront
Fbrake_max_rear = μ × Nrear

μ = tire-road friction coefficient: 0.7–0.9 (dry), 0.3–0.5 (wet). Exceed this and the wheel locks up.

REGEN BRAKING

Regen vs Friction Split

Fregen = min(Fmotor_max, Fbattery_limit, Ftire_limit)
Ffriction = Fbrake_total − Fregen

Regen gets priority (free energy!), friction fills the remainder.

REGEN BRAKING

Regen Force Limits

  • Motor torque limit: Back-EMF limits generating torque at low speeds
  • Battery charge limit: Can't regen if SOC ≈ 100% or charging current exceeds max
  • Tire adhesion: On driven axle only (FWD: front, RWD: rear)
  • Speed threshold: Below ~5 km/h, regen torque too low — friction takes over

REGEN BRAKING

Strategy 1: Fixed Regen

Fregen = m × afixed    (e.g., afixed = 0.1g = 0.981 m/s²)

Simplest approach: constant deceleration when throttle released. Like engine braking in ICE. Used in basic EVs.

REGEN BRAKING

Strategy 2: Proportional (Blended)

Regen force proportional to brake pedal position. Blended braking:

pedal < threshold:   Fregen = Kregen × pedal
pedal ≥ threshold:   Fregen = Fmax;   Ffriction = K(pedal − threshold)

REGEN BRAKING

Strategy 3: Optimal Series Braking

Maximize energy: use all available regen first, then add friction.

  • 1. Calculate total braking force needed
  • 2. Apply maximum regen (limited by motor/battery/tire)
  • 3. Remaining demand → friction brakes

REGEN DAMPER

Derivation: Sprung Mass EOM

Newton's 2nd Law for sprung mass:

ms × z̈s = −ks × (zs − zu) − ctotal × (żs − żu)

Spring force pulls body toward wheel. Damper force opposes relative velocity.

Rearranged: z̈s = −(ks/ms)(zs−zu) − (c/ms)(żs−żu)

REGEN DAMPER

Derivation: Unsprung Mass EOM

Newton's 2nd Law for unsprung mass:

mu × z̈u = ks(zs−zu) + c(żs−żu) − kt(zu−zr)

Spring and damper push wheel down. Tire spring pushes wheel up from road.

Key Takeaways

🔋

SOC Estimation

OCV = snapshot at rest. CC = real-time but drifts. EKF = gold standard (predict + correct). All three are used in production BMS.

♻️

Energy Recovery

Regen braking recovers 15–30% of braking energy. Regen dampers harvest 100–400W from road vibrations. Both improve range and efficiency.
